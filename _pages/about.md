I am a designer and researcher from The Netherlands, residing in the United Kingdom. In my day to day work I design and develop interactive, artificially intelligent systems for use inside the car cabin. My wider research interests focus around "everyday interactions with intelligent cyber-physical systems for non-expert users" (I will explain this in more detail). I obtained degrees in Industrial Design from Eindhoven University of Technology, and Artificial Intelligence from Utrecht University, both in The Netherlands. On this personal website I showcase my public work. You can also find my CV, recent news, as well as links to my other social media accounts. This website is currently a work in progress - more materials will be added along the way. The website works best on a pc or laptop. 

My interest in design originates from a fascination with the objects that surround us in everyday life. Everything man-made was, at some point, invented and created by someone (or most likely by a number of people), to act as a tool for a certain purpose. Ranging from cutlery, to clothing, furniture, and more recently, smartphones, home assistants - all these are displays of human ingenuity, that we own, touch, take into our hands and experience closely throughout our lives.   

Where we have a lot of tools that can do something of a physical or mechanical nature, that we humans can't do (or are unwilling to do), there is also a category of tools that can do something of an intellectual nature - in a sense, human ingenuity has led to us developing tools that can help us in our ingenuity. This is the category of tools that I find most interesting: I call them intelligent cyber-physical systems (because they usually have both a virtual and physical presence). 

Now why would I study interaction between humans and intelligent cyber-physical systems? It turns out that it is quite hard to get the interaction right. In the best case, a suboptimal interaction design might just lead to annoyances or perhaps a slight dip in productivity. In the worst case, a suboptimal interaction design can affect our wellbeing and health, or might even lead to fatalities. 

I am especially interested in design for automotive because of the big importance of good interaction design within this application area. Cars are not only ingenious machines, but also widely used tools by a very large percentage of the world population. At the same time, sadly, a lot of people get injured or worse everyday, from using cars or being close to someone using a car. Then two other key issues are pollution and congestion.

I am glad to see that cars are becoming smarter and better every day, and strongly believe that bringing intelligent systems into vehicles is the right step that we should be taking in order to reduce accidents, pollution and congestions. But in order for us to be able to use these technologies to our benefit, we have to make sure that the interaction is designed in the right way.
